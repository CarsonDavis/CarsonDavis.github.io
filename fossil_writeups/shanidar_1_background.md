## Neanderthal diet (what’s most current, and what’s shifted recently)

Neanderthal diet research is in a really interesting phase: the old “mostly big-game hypercarnivores” view is still supported in many places, but the *interpretation* of key chemical signals is getting more nuanced, and the evidence for flexible, local diets keeps growing.

A big 2025 development is a proposal that the famously high nitrogen-15 (δ¹⁵N) values in many Neanderthals—often read as “they ate like top predators”—may be partly explained by eating **fly larvae (maggots) from stored/fermenting meat**, which can be very δ¹⁵N-enriched. That doesn’t make them “not meat-eaters”; it changes how we read the isotope signal and (importantly) makes meat *storage* and “delayed consumption” part of the behavioral picture. ([Science][1])

Meanwhile, direct residue and microremain evidence continues to show that **plants were not trivial**:

* Dental calculus microfossils provide direct evidence of **starch grains and phytoliths** consistent with plant consumption (and, in some cases, cooking/processing). ([PNAS][2])
* Broader calculus and biomolecular work supports **regional dietary differences**, including plant-heavy signals at some sites and meat-heavy at others, plus hints of “self-medication” (e.g., poplar bark compounds) in at least one individual. ([Nature][3])

Coastal and marine foraging is also looking increasingly solid in some regions. Work at Iberian sites (Portugal/Spain) has added detail on **marine foods** (e.g., crab/shellfish assemblages and interpretation of their accumulation), supporting the view that in some contexts Neanderthals regularly exploited predictable coastal resources rather than only relying on terrestrial big game. ([Frontiers][4])

**What I’d say is the current consensus:** Neanderthals were often *heavily* reliant on animal foods (especially in cold steppe environments), but the best-supported picture is **ecological flexibility**: they ate what was locally abundant and seasonally reliable, and they sometimes processed plant foods in ways that imply planning and know-how. ([Nature][3])

## Tool use and technology (stone, bone, composite tools, and “process” know-how)

Neanderthal stone technology is typically discussed under the Middle Paleolithic / Mousterian umbrella, but what’s most revealing is not the label—it’s the *control*: preparing cores, managing fracture, standardizing products, and maintaining toolkits.

A 2025 experimental paper focuses on **how hammer strike angle affects Levallois flake morphology**, showing that small changes strongly alter fracture trajectories. This matters because it underscores that “precision” doesn’t require magic; it requires **learned control over variables**—the kind of embodied skill that fits well with what lithic specialists infer from archaeological assemblages. ([Springer Link][5])

Neanderthals also made **composite tools** (stone + handle + adhesive). Two especially useful anchor points:

* A ~50,000-year-old **tar-hafted flint tool** demonstrates hafting with adhesive in a way that preserves direct evidence of the composite. ([PNAS][6])
* A 2023 chemical/experimental analysis of the **Königsaue birch tar** argues the tar was produced via an **oxygen-restricted underground distillation method**, implying a multi-step process and learned technique (often discussed in terms of “cumulative culture,” though that framing is debated). ([PMC][7])

On fire: a very recent 2025 line of evidence (Barnham, England) argues for **deliberate fire-making ~400,000 years ago**, likely by *early Neanderthals* or closely related populations in that region/time slice. This is still “hot off the press” and you’ll see careful language in reporting, but if it holds up, it pushes back habitual fire-making dramatically and has huge implications for daily life, diet, and sociality. ([Reuters][8])

## Culture (symbolic behavior, pigments, engravings, ornaments—plus the debates)

“Neanderthal culture” used to be treated as mostly utilitarian. Now the argument is less “did they have culture?” and more “how frequent, transmitted, and socially embedded were their symbolic behaviors compared with Upper Paleolithic H. sapiens?”

A major 2025 contribution is **Science Advances** work on **ochre “crayons”** associated with Crimean Micoquian Neanderthals, interpreting modification and use-wear as evidence for **curated pigment tools** plausibly used for drawing/marking (with appropriate caution about what counts as “art”). ([Science][9])

Two older-but-foundational anchors that still structure the debate:

* **Gorham’s Cave (Gibraltar)**: an abstract cross-hatched engraving associated with Mousterian levels, frequently cited as evidence of non-utilitarian marking behavior. ([PNAS][10])
* **U-Th dating of Spanish cave art carbonate crusts** argued some cave markings predate the arrival of modern humans in Iberia, implying Neanderthal authorship—though the methods and inference chain have been actively debated ever since. ([Science][11])

For a *recent synthetic* take that explicitly reframes “Neanderthal art” around transmission and social connectivity (instead of IQ scoring), see the 2025 open-access review-style discussion in *Evolutionary Human Sciences* / PMC. ([PMC][12])

## Burial practices (what’s strong evidence vs. what’s still contested)

The burial question is one of the most contentious areas because the same basic observations—articulation, sediment infill, pit-like geometry, association with pollen/flowers—can sometimes be produced by non-burial processes in caves.

A widely cited modern taphonomic argument for **intentional burial** is the La Chapelle-aux-Saints case, which lays out evidence consistent with a dug feature and purposeful deposition (while also acknowledging how high the evidentiary bar should be). ([PMC][13])

**Shanidar Cave** remains central, but interpretations have become more careful over time—especially around the famous “flower burial” idea. Newer excavation/analysis has added detail on associations and site formation, while other work has directly challenged the “flowers = ritual burial” inference and pushed for more conservative interpretations grounded in taphonomy and microstratigraphy. ([Archaeology News Online Magazine][14])

**Where the field is landing (roughly):**

* Some Neanderthal deposits look **very** consistent with deliberate burial.
* Many others are ambiguous, and researchers are increasingly strict about separating “possible burial” from “demonstrated burial,” rather than treating all classic claims as equally secure. ([PMC][13])

## Genetic similarity and interbreeding (what we can now say with high confidence)

Instead of a single “percent similar” number (which can mislead), the best-supported modern framing is ancestry and gene flow:

* **Most present-day non-Africans carry ~1–2% Neanderthal ancestry**, inherited from interbreeding after modern humans dispersed out of Africa. (Exact proportions vary by population and method.) ([Reuters][15])
* A 2024 **Science** paper (“Neanderthal ancestry through time”) builds a catalog of Neanderthal-derived segments across many ancient and present-day genomes and uses it to track how selection filtered introgressed DNA over time. ([Science][16])
* High-resolution ancient genomes are tightening *when* mixing happened and how long it persisted. A 2025 **Nature** paper on early modern human genomes discusses detectable Neanderthal ancestry levels (including an estimate around ~2.9% in specific high-coverage ancient individuals) and uses segment lengths/timing to constrain events. ([Nature][17])

A key “so what” from this whole research program: introgression wasn’t just trivia—some Neanderthal-derived variants plausibly affected **immunity, metabolism, and other traits**, and population genetics can now watch those segments rise/fall through time in ancient DNA datasets. ([Science][16])
